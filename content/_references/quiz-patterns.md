# Quiz Patterns

Reusable quiz patterns help authors design diagnostic items without turning quizzes into mini exercise sheets.

Use these patterns as starting points. Adapt them to the target skill, misconception, item type, and remediation path.

## Pattern: choose the method

Purpose:
- Test whether the student can select the appropriate method before calculating.

Best for:
- Method-choice quizzes, exam-readiness checkpoints, and mixed review.

Avoid when:
- The method is obvious from the wording.

Shape:
1. Present a compact task.
2. Ask which method or theorem should be used first.
3. Include distractors for common invalid methods.

Good distractors:
- Valid methods used under the wrong condition.
- Tempting shortcuts that ignore the expression form.

Bad distractors:
- Methods unrelated to the unit.

Diagnostic signal:
- Wrong choice reveals a method-selection weakness.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain the condition or form that points to the correct method.

Review question:
- Does the item test method choice before computation?

## Pattern: identify the invalid step

Purpose:
- Detect whether the student can audit a solution.

Best for:
- Error-clinic quizzes and theorem-condition traps.

Avoid when:
- Every step is valid but stylistically imperfect.

Shape:
1. Show a short student solution.
2. Label the steps.
3. Ask which step is invalid and why.

Good distractors:
- Steps that look suspicious but are valid.
- A later step that only inherits the earlier error.

Bad distractors:
- Obviously nonsensical steps.

Diagnostic signal:
- Choice reveals whether the student sees the real mathematical failure.

Feedback requirement:
- Name the first invalid step and the condition it violates.

Review question:
- Is there exactly one first invalid step?

## Pattern: choose all theorem conditions

Purpose:
- Check complete understanding of a theorem's conditions.

Best for:
- Multiple-response items on continuity, differentiability, TVI, probability, or geometry theorems.

Avoid when:
- Partial correctness rules are unclear.

Shape:
1. Name the theorem or situation.
2. List possible conditions.
3. Ask for all required conditions.

Good distractors:
- Conditions from related theorems.
- Stronger-than-needed conditions.

Bad distractors:
- Random vocabulary.

Diagnostic signal:
- Missed or extra conditions reveal theorem confusion.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain selected wrong choices and missed correct choices.

Review question:
- Can the item distinguish "knows the theorem name" from "knows how to use it"?

## Pattern: match expression to method

Purpose:
- Test recognition of algebraic or analytic forms.

Best for:
- Limits, derivatives, primitives, logarithms, exponentials, and complex numbers.

Avoid when:
- Multiple methods are equally natural and not documented.

Shape:
1. List expressions.
2. List methods.
3. Match each expression to the best first method.

Good distractors:
- Methods that work for nearby forms.

Bad distractors:
- Methods never used in the unit.

Diagnostic signal:
- Wrong pairs reveal form-recognition gaps.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain the visual/form cue in each expression.

Review question:
- Is the method cue visible in the expression itself?

## Pattern: order proof steps

Purpose:
- Test proof or method dependency.

Best for:
- Sequence items and exam method ordering.

Avoid when:
- Several orders are valid.

Shape:
1. Give a goal.
2. Provide unordered steps.
3. Ask for a valid order.

Good distractors:
- Swapped steps that break a dependency.

Bad distractors:
- Tiny algebra moves that do not matter.

Diagnostic signal:
- Wrong order reveals missing method logic.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain why each step depends on the previous one.

Review question:
- Does the sequence test reasoning order, not memory?

## Pattern: complete the missing step

Purpose:
- Check whether the student can bridge a key transformation.

Best for:
- Fill-blank, missing-step, and micro-calculation roles.

Avoid when:
- The missing step has many equivalent forms and alternatives are not listed.

Shape:
1. Show a short partial solution.
2. Leave one meaningful blank.
3. Ask for the missing expression or reason.

Good distractors:
- Common algebra slips or condition omissions.

Bad distractors:
- Random expressions.

Diagnostic signal:
- Answer reveals whether the transformation is understood.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain the transformation and accepted alternatives.

Review question:
- Is the blank the important step, not a cosmetic detail?

## Pattern: classify the form

Purpose:
- Test recognition before action.

Best for:
- Limits, sequences, derivatives, integrals, and probability situations.

Avoid when:
- Classification does not change the next method.

Shape:
1. Present an expression or situation.
2. Ask for the form/type.
3. Route to the matching method.

Good distractors:
- Nearby forms that require different methods.

Bad distractors:
- Labels not used in the course.

Diagnostic signal:
- Wrong classification predicts wrong method choice.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain the cue that determines the class.

Review question:
- Does classification lead to a different next action?

## Pattern: choose the best next action

Purpose:
- Diagnose procedural strategy after a partial result.

Best for:
- Method-choice and exam-readiness quizzes.

Avoid when:
- Any next action would work equally well.

Shape:
1. Show current work.
2. Ask what to do next.
3. Include tempting but premature actions.

Good distractors:
- Jumping to a theorem before conditions.
- Substitution before simplification.

Bad distractors:
- Actions that no student would consider.

Diagnostic signal:
- Choice reveals planning and sequencing weakness.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain why this is the next safe step.

Review question:
- Is the next action determined by the current mathematical state?

## Pattern: find the counterexample

Purpose:
- Test conceptual precision.

Best for:
- True/false with reason and misconception traps.

Avoid when:
- Counterexamples are too advanced for the target level.

Shape:
1. Give a false general claim.
2. Offer possible counterexamples.
3. Ask which one disproves the claim.

Good distractors:
- Examples that satisfy the claim.
- Examples that fail for the wrong reason.

Bad distractors:
- Impossible objects.

Diagnostic signal:
- Choice reveals whether the student understands universal claims.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain how a single counterexample breaks the claim.

Review question:
- Does the counterexample target the exact false condition?

## Pattern: diagnose student solution

Purpose:
- Identify the misconception behind a wrong solution.

Best for:
- Error-clinic and feedback-rich MCQ items.

Avoid when:
- The shown solution has multiple independent errors.

Shape:
1. Show a concise student answer.
2. Ask what the student's mistake is.
3. Offer misconception labels or corrections.

Good distractors:
- Real nearby misconceptions.

Bad distractors:
- Vague labels such as "calculation error" when the issue is conceptual.

Diagnostic signal:
- Choice reveals whether the learner can name the error.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain the mistake and the repair.

Review question:
- Is the shown student solution realistic?

## Pattern: graph-to-conclusion

Purpose:
- Test reading information from a graph, table, or variation chart.

Best for:
- Graph-reading roles and hotspot-ready items.

Avoid when:
- The visual data is not precise enough.

Shape:
1. Provide or describe the visual.
2. Ask for the conclusion.
3. Include wrong conclusions from common visual misreadings.

Good distractors:
- Endpoint confusion.
- Sign/variation confusion.

Bad distractors:
- Conclusions unrelated to the visual.

Diagnostic signal:
- Wrong answer reveals visual interpretation weakness.

Feedback requirement:
- Point to the exact visual clue.

Review question:
- Can the answer be derived from the visual alone?

## Pattern: statement true/false with reason

Purpose:
- Test a precise conceptual claim and its reason.

Best for:
- Quick misconception checks.

Avoid when:
- Guessing without a reason would be accepted.

Shape:
1. Give a precise statement.
2. Ask true/false.
3. Require the reason or correction.

Good distractors:
- Reasons that sound close but violate a condition.

Bad distractors:
- Pure wording tricks.

Diagnostic signal:
- Wrong answer or wrong reason reveals the misconception.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain the missing condition or correction.

Review question:
- Is the reason more important than the binary label?

## Pattern: select required domain condition

Purpose:
- Test whether the student checks domain before using a formula or theorem.

Best for:
- Logarithms, square roots, rational functions, limits, derivatives, and integrals.

Avoid when:
- The domain condition is irrelevant to the target skill.

Shape:
1. Present a formula, expression, or theorem use.
2. Ask which domain condition is required.
3. Include tempting incomplete conditions.

Good distractors:
- Conditions that are necessary but incomplete.
- Conditions from a similar expression.

Bad distractors:
- Random inequalities.

Diagnostic signal:
- Wrong condition reveals domain-checking weakness.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain where the condition comes from.

Review question:
- Is the domain condition mathematically necessary?

## Pattern: exam chain checkpoint

Purpose:
- Check one decision point inside an exam-style chain.

Best for:
- Exam-readiness quizzes after supporting content exists.

Avoid when:
- The item copies a full exam exercise or makes unsupported exam-frequency claims.

Shape:
1. Present one short moment from an exam-like chain.
2. Ask for the next safe conclusion.
3. Route wrong answers to targeted remediation.

Good distractors:
- Common exam traps.
- Premature conclusions.

Bad distractors:
- Full-exercise answer choices.

Diagnostic signal:
- Choice reveals whether the student can continue an exam method.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain the local exam reflex without claiming official frequency unless sourced.

Review question:
- Is this an original exam-pattern checkpoint, not copied exam content?

## Pattern: misconception trap

Purpose:
- Surface one high-value misconception directly.

Best for:
- Error-clinic, prerequisite, and concept-recognition quizzes.

Avoid when:
- The trap feels like a trick instead of a real student mistake.

Shape:
1. Present a situation where the misconception is tempting.
2. Include the misconception as an answer.
3. Contrast it with the correct reasoning.

Good distractors:
- The exact tempting false conclusion.

Bad distractors:
- Artificial traps no student would choose.

Diagnostic signal:
- Selection identifies a misconception to remediate.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain why the trap is tempting and what condition prevents it.

Review question:
- Is the trap pedagogically useful, not punitive?

## Pattern: compare two answers

Purpose:
- Test reasoning quality by comparing two proposed answers.

Best for:
- Conceptual precision and answer-key review practice.

Avoid when:
- Both answers differ only by notation preference.

Shape:
1. Give two student answers.
2. Ask which is correct, or whether both/none are correct.
3. Ask for the decisive reason.

Good distractors:
- Answers that are numerically close but conceptually different.

Bad distractors:
- One obviously impossible answer.

Diagnostic signal:
- Choice reveals whether the student can judge reasoning, not only compute.

Feedback requirement:
- Explain the decisive condition or step.

Review question:
- Is the comparison meaningful for the target skill?

## Pattern: partial reasoning check

Purpose:
- Diagnose an incomplete but not fully wrong reasoning path.

Best for:
- Multiple-response, partially-correct feedback, and remediation routing.

Avoid when:
- The system cannot represent partial correctness clearly.

Shape:
1. Show a partial argument.
2. Ask what is valid, missing, or invalid.
3. Give feedback by component.

Good distractors:
- Valid steps missing a condition.
- Correct conclusion with incomplete justification.

Bad distractors:
- All-or-nothing answers when partial signal matters.

Diagnostic signal:
- Response distinguishes fragile understanding from full mastery.

Feedback requirement:
- State what is correct, what is missing, and how to complete it.

Review question:
- Does the item separate partial understanding from misconception?
