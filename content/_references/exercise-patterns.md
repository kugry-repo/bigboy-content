# Exercise Patterns

Reusable patterns help design exercises that build ability rather than just add quantity.

## Pattern: Direct application

Purpose:
- Train a newly introduced definition, formula, theorem, or routine.

Best for:
- First core-skill practice after a mini-lesson.

Avoid when:
- The method is so obvious that the exercise adds no decision or fluency value.

Shape:
1. Present a clean statement.
2. Ask for one targeted calculation or conclusion.
3. Require a final result and a quick check.

Typical traps:
- Using the formula without checking its conditions.

Good review question:
- Does this exercise train one precise skill, or is it only extra arithmetic?

## Pattern: Method choice

Purpose:
- Train recognition and selection between plausible tools.

Best for:
- Limits, function study, equations, counting models, or geometry problems with several possible approaches.

Avoid when:
- The statement names the method directly.

Shape:
1. Give a statement where at least two methods seem possible.
2. Ask the student to solve and justify the method.
3. In the solution, explain why the chosen method fits.

Typical traps:
- Applying the most recent method instead of reading the structure.

Good review question:
- What exactly must the student notice before starting?

## Pattern: Wrong solution correction

Purpose:
- Turn a common error into recovery training.

Best for:
- Domain errors, false theorem use, invalid simplification, probability condition confusion.

Avoid when:
- The wrong solution is artificial or too silly.

Shape:
1. Present a short flawed solution.
2. Ask the student to find the first wrong step.
3. Ask for a corrected solution or corrected reflex.

Typical traps:
- Naming the error without explaining why it was tempting.

Good review question:
- Would a real student plausibly make this mistake?

## Pattern: Fill missing step

Purpose:
- Train the exact bridge between two lines of reasoning.

Best for:
- Algebraic transformations, proof steps, theorem-condition checks.

Avoid when:
- The missing step is pure transcription.

Shape:
1. Give a partial solution.
2. Leave one meaningful step blank.
3. Ask for the step and the reason.

Typical traps:
- Writing a result without the condition that permits it.

Good review question:
- Does the missing step reveal a real skill?

## Pattern: Compare two methods

Purpose:
- Build method awareness and efficiency.

Best for:
- Limits, derivatives, equations, probability counting.

Avoid when:
- The two methods are not genuinely comparable.

Shape:
1. Solve or outline two methods.
2. Compare their conditions and effort.
3. Ask which method is preferable and why.

Typical traps:
- Treating both methods as equally valid without checking assumptions.

Good review question:
- Does the comparison teach a future decision?

## Pattern: Prove or disprove

Purpose:
- Train proof discipline and counterexample thinking.

Best for:
- Continuity claims, monotonicity claims, set inclusions, probability independence.

Avoid when:
- A direct calculation is the real target.

Shape:
1. State a claim.
2. Ask whether it is true.
3. Require proof or a counterexample.

Typical traps:
- Verifying one example and treating it as proof.

Good review question:
- Is the claim precise enough to be true or false?

## Pattern: Classify cases

Purpose:
- Train recognizing when a parameter, sign, or domain changes the method.

Best for:
- Parameters, inequalities, absolute values, sequences, probability cases.

Avoid when:
- Cases are artificial and do not change the reasoning.

Shape:
1. Present a family or parameter.
2. Ask for classification by case.
3. Justify transitions between cases.

Typical traps:
- Forgetting a boundary case.

Good review question:
- Are all cases necessary and non-overlapping?

## Pattern: Parameter exercise

Purpose:
- Make conditions visible by varying a parameter.

Best for:
- Existence, uniqueness, sign, tangent, counting, probability law.

Avoid when:
- Parameter values only make arithmetic longer.

Shape:
1. Define a family depending on a parameter.
2. Ask for the values that satisfy a condition.
3. Check edge cases.

Typical traps:
- Dividing by an expression that can be zero.

Good review question:
- Does the parameter reveal a condition or only decorate the exercise?

## Pattern: Graph interpretation

Purpose:
- Train reading mathematical information from a representation.

Best for:
- Limits, continuity, variation, signs, derivatives.

Avoid when:
- The graph is not available or is too ambiguous.

Shape:
1. Provide or describe a graph/table.
2. Ask for readings and conclusions.
3. Ask for at least one justification from the representation.

Typical traps:
- Confusing value, limit, and image.

Good review question:
- Can the student answer from the graph without hidden data?

## Pattern: Complete a variation/sign table

Purpose:
- Train organizing derivative or sign information.

Best for:
- Function study and inequalities.

Avoid when:
- The table is mechanical and the derivative sign is already given without purpose.

Shape:
1. Give a function or partial information.
2. Ask for sign or variation table completion.
3. Use the table for a conclusion.

Typical traps:
- Losing domain breaks or critical values.

Good review question:
- Does the table support a real later conclusion?

## Pattern: Theorem-condition check

Purpose:
- Train applying a theorem only when conditions are met.

Best for:
- Continuity, TVI, derivability, probability independence, geometry criteria.

Avoid when:
- The theorem conditions are automatic and not part of the target.

Shape:
1. Present a theorem-use situation.
2. Ask the student to check conditions.
3. Apply or refuse the theorem.

Typical traps:
- Naming the theorem without verifying hypotheses.

Good review question:
- Which condition is easiest to forget?

## Pattern: Exam chain

Purpose:
- Train a reusable multi-step chain seen in exam-style work.

Best for:
- Limit to continuity, derivative to variation, counting to probability, complex form changes.

Avoid when:
- The exercise claims official frequency without source.

Shape:
1. Start with a recognition step.
2. Build two or three linked results.
3. Conclude with a decision or final value.

Typical traps:
- Treating intermediate results as isolated calculations.

Good review question:
- Is the chain useful beyond this exact statement?

## Pattern: Synthesis problem

Purpose:
- Combine skills while keeping the path learnable.

Best for:
- End-of-cluster or end-of-unit practice.

Avoid when:
- Prerequisites have not been trained separately.

Shape:
1. Present a compact multi-part problem.
2. Each part unlocks the next.
3. Finish with a meaningful conclusion.

Typical traps:
- Overloading the student with unrelated tasks.

Good review question:
- Does each part serve the final goal?

## Pattern: Reverse-engineer condition

Purpose:
- Train working backward from a desired result.

Best for:
- Parameter choice, tangent conditions, continuity, probability constraints.

Avoid when:
- The backward path is too opaque for the target difficulty.

Shape:
1. State the desired property.
2. Ask for parameters or assumptions that make it true.
3. Verify the found values.

Typical traps:
- Solving a necessary condition and forgetting to verify sufficiency.

Good review question:
- Does the solution check that the condition really works?

## Pattern: Minimal counterexample

Purpose:
- Train precision by finding the smallest example that breaks a false claim.

Best for:
- Definitions, implications, monotonicity, continuity, independence.

Avoid when:
- The course has not yet developed enough examples.

Shape:
1. State a false or suspicious claim.
2. Ask for a simple counterexample.
3. Explain exactly which part fails.

Typical traps:
- Giving a complicated example that hides the reason.

Good review question:
- Is the counterexample simple enough to teach the concept?
