# Lesson Quality Rubric

## Purpose

This rubric is used to review student-facing lessons.

A lesson should not be considered `reviewed` if it is correct but dry, or friendly but mathematically weak.

A strong lesson should combine:

- motivation;
- intuition;
- mathematical precision;
- examples;
- interactivity;
- exam usefulness;
- readability.

## Scoring

Use a score from 1 to 5 for each criterion.

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Missing or poor |
| 2 | Present but weak |
| 3 | Acceptable draft |
| 4 | Good |
| 5 | Excellent |

A lesson should not be marked `reviewed` if any critical criterion is below 4.

Critical criteria:

- Mathematical correctness.
- Conditions and rigor.
- Intuition before formalism.
- Readability.
- Examples and methods.

## Rubric

| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Motivation / why it matters | 1-5 | Does the lesson explain why the concept exists? |
| Intuition before formalism | 1-5 | Does the lesson build the idea before the formal definition? |
| Mathematical correctness | 1-5 | Are statements, examples, and methods correct? |
| Conditions and rigor | 1-5 | Are theorem conditions and domain restrictions explicit? |
| Early concrete example | 1-5 | Does the first example appear early enough? |
| Mental model | 1-5 | Does the lesson give the student a useful way to think about the concept? |
| Analogy quality | 1-5 | Are analogies helpful, not forced, and reconnected to math? |
| Concept contrast | 1-5 | Are common confusions clarified? |
| Student interaction | 1-5 | Are there prediction moments, mini-checks, or pause points? |
| Common mistakes | 1-5 | Are traps shown directly and corrected? |
| Exam usefulness | 1-5 | Are exam reflexes included without dominating the lesson? |
| Readability | 1-5 | Are paragraphs short, clear, and well-paced? |
| Voice consistency | 1-5 | Does the lesson feel like a friendly mentor, not a dry handout? |
| Visual thinking | 1-5 | Are diagrams, graphs, or visual ideas suggested when useful? |

## Review questions

Before marking a lesson as `reviewed`, ask:

1. Does the lesson start with a reason, question, problem, or intuition?
2. Does it answer "why are we studying this?"
3. Does a concrete example appear early?
4. Are important concepts explained in human language before formal language?
5. Are formal statements still precise?
6. Are theorem conditions explicit?
7. Are there no unexplained "magic steps"?
8. Are analogies useful and reconnected to the math?
9. Are likely student confusions addressed?
10. Are common traps shown directly?
11. Are exam reflexes included?
12. Are shortcuts clearly labeled?
13. Does the lesson use `tu`?
14. Does the lesson end with `La carte mentale`?
15. Would a student likely say: "I understand the idea and I know what to do in exercises"?

## Minimum standard for a reviewed lesson

A reviewed lesson should have:

- A clear `Pourquoi on étudie ça ?` section.
- At least one early concrete example.
- Mini-lessons with visible progression.
- Human explanation before formal statement.
- Explicit mathematical conditions.
- At least one checkpoint or prediction moment.
- Common mistakes near the relevant concepts.
- A `Comment ça tombe à l'examen ?` section.
- A final `La carte mentale` summary.
